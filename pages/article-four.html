Introduction to Cells:

The Building Blocks of Life

What Are Cells?

Have you ever wondered what makes up every living thing on Earth—including you? The answer is cells. These microscopic units are the smallest structures capable of performing all the functions of life. Whether it’s a blade of grass, a buzzing bee, or a human body, cells are the foundation. In fact, your body contains around 37 trillion cells

Cell Components and Their Functions

Essential Structures

Organelle Function
Cell Membrane Phospholipid bilayer that controls what enters/leaves the cell and maintains homeostasis
Nucleus Contains genetic material (DNA) and controls cellular activities
Cytoplasm Jelly-like substance where organelles are suspended and many metabolic reactions occur

Energy Production

Organelle Function
Mitochondria Powerhouse of the cell - produces ATP through cellular respiration
Chloroplasts In plant cells - perform photosynthesis to convert sunlight into chemical energy

Protein Synthesis

Organelle Function
Ribosomes Site of protein synthesis (some free in cytoplasm, others attached to ER)
Endoplasmic Reticulum Rough ER (with ribosomes) makes proteins; Smooth ER makes lipids and detoxifies
Golgi Apparatus Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for secretion or delivery

Other Important Structures

Additional cell components include:

  • Lysosomes - Contain digestive enzymes to break down waste
  • Vacuoles - Storage sacs (especially large in plant cells)
  • Cytoskeleton - Network of fibers that maintains cell shape
  • Centrioles - Help organize cell division (animal cells only)
  • Cell Wall - Rigid outer layer (plant cells only)

Microscope Activity:

  1. Prepare a wet mount slide (onion skin or cheek cells work well)
  2. Start with lowest magnification (40x)
  3. Focus and identify cell structures
  4. Increase magnification to 100x then 400x
  5. Sketch and label what you observe
Microscope Safety:
  • Always carry microscopes with two hands
  • Never use the coarse focus knob on high power
  • Clean slides and lenses with lens paper only
  • Dispose of biological samples properly
  • Wash hands after handling slides

Remember: Many cell structures are too small to see with light microscopes - electron microscopes are needed to view organelles in detail!
